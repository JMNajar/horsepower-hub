Executive Summary

Everything in this strategy flows from one lens: Easyriders was built on sex, drugs, and rock and roll, interpreted for a modern membership. That's not a marketing bolt-on — it's the brand's actual reason for being, and Section 01 makes that case before anything else in this document.

The honest version of "go after Gen Z" is not "sell Gen Z a bagger." Gen Z's actual buying behavior skews toward cheap, light, used standard bikes — not the big-twin custom and cruiser world Easyriders has spent 55 years mythologizing — and a real share of them believe motorcycles are simply too dangerous to bother with. That is a demand-side reality no amount of content will reverse this year.

What Easyriders can actually do — and what the channel's own numbers already prove is working — is recruit Gen Z as audience and culture-carriers now, while the near-term commercial opportunity sits with Gen X and Millennial buyers who have the garage space and income for customs. That is not a consolation prize; it is the same playbook Hagerty ran turning an insurance company into an 800,000-member car club, and it is a playbook built almost entirely from things Easyriders already has: a 55-year archive nobody else owns, real relationships with the people who made the mythology (Barger's world, Dave Mann's art, working builder shops), and a channel where the single highest-converting video to date is Keith talking straight to camera about the brand changing.

The strategic bet in this document: Easyriders stops competing as a magazine and starts operating as a cultural membership organization with a media arm — print, video, events, and merchandise as channels, not the product. The archive is the moat. Personalities beat production value. And every recommendation below is checked against what the channel's own May–July 2026 data already shows, not generic social media theory.

03

Content That Earns Attention

The channel already ran the experiment. The data says personalities and stakes beat production value — every recommendation here extends what's already winning, rather than guessing at Gen Z theory from scratch.

What already stopped the scroll

"Easyriders Is Changing: And Not Everyone Likes It" — unscripted, Keith talking straight to camera about real internal conflict — produced the channel's highest subscriber conversion of anything published to date. "I Bought Easyriders… We Found the Captain America Bike!" — legacy IP wrapped in a personal discovery story — produced the highest raw views (14K). The long-form Vault archival series gets fewer views but the strongest watch time, meaning it's doing a different job: deepening an already-hooked viewer, not acquiring a new one.

Content ranked by stop-scroll power

  1. Personalities in conflict or transformation — a builder, an owner, Bandit himself, taking a real position. Proven top performer.
  2. Origin-story / mystery framing of a legendary object — a bike, a piece of art, a name, revealed like a discovery, not a spec sheet.
  3. Build documentation as character study — the build is the backdrop; the builder's decisions and stakes are the story.
  4. History reframed as "how today's look got invented" — the direct line from Dave Mann's art and 1970s outlaw runs to 2026 patch culture and chopper-core.
  5. Events as proof of culture — not b-roll, but evidence that a real community exists and shows up.
  6. Technical instruction — lowest priority. Oversaturated by OEM and enthusiast-retail channels, and it's not what wins on Easyriders' own numbers.

Lifestyle content works as connective tissue and thumbnail material — it rarely wins standing on its own.

The archive is repackagable, and almost nobody else has one

Fifty-five years of Dave Mann art, Sonny Barger-era mythology, and outlaw-run photography is raw material for a serialized "how this became mainstream" documentary thread — a direct line from 1970s Easyriders pages to the chopper-core and vintage-flash-tattoo aesthetic already trending in custom culture today. This is a genuine moat: the content format can be copied by any new channel in months; the archive cannot.

Modern builders, women riders, and diverse communities

This isn't a diversity checkbox — it's chasing the fastest-growing segment in the sport. Women are 19% of all U.S. owners and 26% of Millennial owners, a meaningfully higher share than the overall average. Groups like The Litas (300+ chapters, open to women, trans women, and non-binary riders) and builders like Krystal Hess are already proving demand for this content. The recommendation is integration, not a separate vertical: feature builders and riders by merit in the same feed as Barger and Dave Mann, the way the channel already does — credibility comes from normalization, not a segregated bucket.

What's worth paying for when content is free

Not the content — the channel's own numbers show 90.7% of traffic already arrives free, through YouTube's own algorithm. What's payable is access: physical and collectible objects tied to a 55-year archive nothing else has (Dave Mann prints, vault issues), real proximity to builders and events, and a membership identity — the same logic that turned Hagerty's magazine into one benefit among many inside an 800,000-member Drivers Club, rather than the product itself. See the companion Hagerty Model brief for the full mechanics — and where Easyriders should deliberately not follow Hagerty's lead.

One feature, six outputs

Easyriders is already doing an early version of this manually — Dave Nichols' Monday YouTube-to-email pipeline, and the newly greenlit QR-code-in-print-to-video loop. The recommendation is to formalize it: every commissioned print feature gets scoped at commission time for video, Shorts, social cutdowns, email, merch, and event tie-in — not retrofitted after the fact.

04

Media Behavior & Platforms

Where they are, what length they'll watch, and what actually moves someone from viewer to email to buyer.

Platform reality

YouTube has the broadest daily engagement across all four generations — which validates the current channel investment directly. For Gen Z specifically, Instagram (58% daily) and TikTok (56% daily) run close behind, and TikTok has become a primary discovery and news source for that generation, ahead of traditional news apps. The right shape: TikTok and Reels for discovery ("did you know" archive-history cuts), YouTube for depth once someone's already curious.

Long-form vs. short-form

Gen Z defaults to short-form — north of 90% watch it daily, averaging two-plus hours. But that doesn't mean they reject long-form: Easyriders' own Vault series proves strong watch time once someone's already inside the funnel. The pattern to design around: short-form acquires, long-form retains.

Viewer → email → buyer

Generic "sign up for our newsletter" won't convert this audience. "Unlock the next part of this story" will — which is exactly the mechanism behind the QR-code test already greenlit with Keith. The subscriber-to-buyer jump isn't won with a discount; it's won with identity and belonging. People join the Hagerty Drivers Club to belong to the world's largest paid car club, not to save money on a magazine.

People, not mastheads

Gen Z trusts creators and peers over brands — which the channel has already proven on its own: the single best-performing video is Keith, unscripted, speaking directly to camera. Bandit, Dave Nichols, and featured builders need to be the visible faces of every platform, not the Easyriders logo alone.

Cadence

Weekly long-form (the current Monday cadence with Dave Nichols) is right for YouTube. Short-form needs to run far more often — daily or near-daily — since it's nearly free to produce from existing footage and short-form platforms reward frequency directly.

05

Community & Events

Younger riders are showing up to events — just not the events the industry built for their parents.

The rally/attendance model is aging with its audience. What's actually growing is the maker-and-show scene — Handbuilt Show (Austin and Long Beach) and Wheels & Waves are drawing real Millennial and Gen Z interest, amplified directly by Instagram and TikTok exposure of the builds themselves. The pull is proximity to the maker and the object, and something worth filming — a builder demonstration delivers both; a concert or a party delivers neither as reliably.

Where community should actually live

Digital-first, physical as the upsell. A lower-friction digital community (Discord-style — already gaining ground with younger audiences as community-driven space) should be the entry point; a physical Easyriders chapter is a deepening layer for the already-converted, not the front door.

What makes them bring a friend

Something filmable and postable, and access that confers status — backstage or builder proximity, not spectator seating.

VerdictThis is Easyriders' most defensible membership lever. Exclusive access to real builders, bikes, the archive, and Bandit himself is a real-world experience no individual creator or media brand can reproduce — 55 years of relationships aren't a content format, they're an asset.

Authentic language, not "trying too hard"

Let the real personalities speak in their own voice on camera instead of writing "youth" copy. Gen Z's radar for a legacy brand performing youth is highly tuned and punishes it fast — avoid slang, trend-chasing captions, and forced meme formats. The proof this works is already on the channel: the top performer is unscripted.

New contributors without sanitizing the brand

"Did Easyriders Find Its New Dave Mann?" is exactly the right instinct — introduce new talent as the next chapter of an existing lineage, not a replacement for it.

Is the archive an active commercial asset today?

Only partially. It's used reactively for content — the Vault series, "Back in the Day" — but it isn't yet productized: no searchable archive product, no systematic licensed-merch pipeline, no standing monetization plan. This is the single most underexploited asset in the business.

Could a competitor duplicate this?

The content format, yes — within months. The archive, the relationships, and 55 years of accumulated trust, no. The moat is the asset base, not the production style, which is exactly why competing purely on content polish would be fighting on the wrong ground.

VerdictStop competing as a magazine. Compete as a cultural membership organization with a media arm — print, video, events, and merch are channels; belonging to the culture Easyriders originated is the product.
08

The Economics

Some of this can be answered today from the channel's own data. The rest shouldn't be guessed at — it should be instrumented.

What we already know

Blended acquisition cost for a viewer is already close to zero — 90.7% of May 2026 traffic came from YouTube's own Home Feed algorithm, with only 0.5% from any paid or external push. The channel is not the bottleneck. The bottleneck is everything downstream of the view: there is currently no systematic viewer-to-email mechanism, and the newsletter list itself is still built manually.

What shouldn't be answered with invented numbers

Viewer-to-email conversion, email-to-buyer conversion, first purchase value, and annual customer value all require a real baseline that doesn't exist yet for Easyriders specifically. The QR-code-to-video loop already greenlit with Keith is the first instrumented test — treat it as the source of the first real numbers, not a placeholder for numbers assumed in advance.

Likely margin ranking (directional, to be tested)

  1. Sponsorships — monetizes attention already owned, no incremental production cost.
  2. Membership / events — the Hagerty pattern; recurring, identity-driven.
  3. Merchandise — Dave Mann prints and apparel tied to owned IP.
  4. Digital archive access — a new product, not yet built.
  5. Subscription — legacy strength, but the weakest unit economics of any legacy print business today.
  6. Single issue — lowest margin, mainly a discovery product now.

Multiple revenue surfaces per feature

The "one feature, six outputs" model from Section 03 isn't just a content strategy — each output (video placement, merch tie-in, event appearance, sponsor mention) is a separate revenue surface generated from a single production cost already being paid for.

VerdictDon't commit to a "profitable at X customers by Y date" number yet. Frame it to stakeholders honestly: instrumentation starts now, first real unit economics land after one full quarter of QR-code and email-funnel data — targeted for Q4 2026.
09

Protecting the Core

The existing audience isn't an obstacle to this strategy — done right, they're the ambassadors who make it credible.

Why they stay

Proximity to the real mythology and the feeling of being early and original — something no shinier, younger competitor can replicate by definition.

What would read as abandonment

  • Replacing legacy voices (Dave Nichols, Keith himself) instead of adding new ones alongside them
  • Changing the visual identity or tone rather than just the presentation layer
  • Chasing slang or trend formats instead of letting real personalities speak
  • Deprioritizing print in favor of digital

One brand, two audiences

Hagerty already proved the model: traditional collectors and younger enthusiasts under one club and one media umbrella, with differentiated series and offers rather than a second brand. Easyriders' legacy-facing content (deep Vault, print-forward) should continue untouched while new series run alongside it — addition, not replacement.

Legacy subscribers as ambassadors

An underused lever. The existing base holds relationships and stories current production doesn't have on rotation — the Deadwood show alone proves those community ties are still live. Recruit longtime readers as on-camera contributors and hosts for archive content, not just as subscribers.

VerdictModernization expands the audience only if it's executed as addition. It divides the audience the moment Easyriders removes or replaces anything flagged untouchable in Section 6. Treat "Not Everyone Likes It" as an early warning signal worth monitoring, not a headline to be proud of on its own.

The Giveaway Decision

Two questions had to be settled before any entry copy could be written: who is this prize legally allowed to reach, and which prize format actually gets promoted. Both are resolved below.

The rule that does the targeting for you

Entry is limited to active Easyriders subscribers — there is no free or alternative entry path. That single rule does more targeting work than any creative decision could: it doesn't exclude any generation by name, but it has the effect of ruling one out entirely.

Ride vs. custom bike — the decision matrix

VerdictThe ride wins on both axes, and it isn't close. A custom bike carries real emotional weight, but it's a multi-year content commitment — roughly two years of build-progress footage and builder profiles before the promotion pays off, with real risk of interest cooling over that runway. A ride can be explained in one sentence, promoted through every channel already in use, and produces its own content the moment it happens. Recommendation: run the ride as the subscription-driving giveaway. If a custom build still gets made, position it separately as a flagship legacy project — not the vehicle for moving the subscription needle.
