Gates, Timeline & Launch Readiness

The calendar estimates; the gates decide. Eight weeks is the floor — valid only if the inputs are substantially complete before the clock starts.

← Back to the Working Brief

The Five Decision Gates

Dates predict; gates authorize. Nothing advances on schedule pressure — it advances when the gate's criteria are met, in writing.

GatePasses WhenAuthorizes
G0 — StartLaunch SKUs named (~6, already packaged or nearly complete) · retail + subscription pricing · COGS per SKU · packaging status per SKU · product data sheets (ingredients, dosing, warnings, COAs) · photography in hand or shoot scheduled · formulator bio + headshot deliveredThe build clock. Until G0 passes, the 8-week timeline hasn't started, whatever the calendar says.
G1 — BuildStorefront name locked (domain, handles, trademark knockout) · the full payments checklist below answered in writing · fulfillment owner signed (in-house or 3PL) · build + acquisition-test budgets approvedFull page production and app-stack configuration.
G2 — ContentClaims sign-off on every page and every flow email · support system live (owner named, SLA set, refund policy written, adverse-reaction protocol documented) · consent management configuredAnything becoming public.
G3 — Soft LaunchReal-card transactions succeed on every launch SKU · subscription lifecycle tested (signup, skip, cancel, refund) · dashboard reporting live dataWarm-list launch (~2,000 contacts, sector-matched drops over 5 days). Advance threshold: ≥1.5% list-to-purchase and <2% refund rate over 14 days.
G4 — Paid TrafficG3 thresholds met · CAC ceiling computed from real margin data · creative batch readyPaid testing: one sector first, 3–5 creatives, fixed daily budget, 14 days. Scale when CAC beats the ceiling two consecutive weeks. Pause when CAC exceeds 1.5× ceiling for 7 days, or chargebacks approach the processor's threshold. The second sector gets budget only after the first proves.

Later gates (post-launch): the Phase 2 gate (remaining owned SKUs + fourth sector) opens on sustained repeat-purchase and cross-sector data; the wholesale gate (B2B portal + CRM enters the stack) opens on D2C unit economics proving out; the marketplace gate (third-party brands, revenue-share) opens last, and only with a signed agreement template ready.

The takeaway: Gate 1 is a warm-list problem, Gate 2 is a subscription problem, Gate 3 is a paid-traffic problem, Gate 4 is a wholesale problem. Each gate has one dominant lever — which tells the marketing engine what to optimize at every stage.

The Payments Checklist (Expanded — Per SKU, Not Per Store)

"Processor confirmed" is too narrow for this catalog. Seven answers, in writing, before G1:

  1. Product eligibility per SKU — kava AND every cannabinoid product ruled on explicitly by the processor, not assumed.
  2. Reserve terms — will the processor hold a rolling reserve, and how much of revenue does it trap?
  3. Chargeback rules — what rate triggers review or termination, and what's the dispute process?
  4. Subscription processing — recurring billing on these product categories explicitly permitted.
  5. State-by-state restrictions — which SKUs cannot sell or ship into which states (kava, cannabinoids, methylene blue).
  6. Shipping limitations — carrier rules for hemp/cannabinoid products; confirmed lanes per SKU.
  7. Backup processor — a second option signed up before launch, not shopped for after a shutdown notice.

The Support System (Required Before G2)

Wellness products require a defined response system — this is a launch gate, not a nice-to-have.

ScenarioResponse Path
Product questions (dosing, ingredients, interactions)Macro library written from the product data sheets; anything beyond the macros escalates to the formulator — support never improvises answers about ingestibles
Subscription changes (skip, swap, pause, cancel)Self-serve first in the customer portal; ticket fallback with same-day handling — subscription friction is churn
RefundsWritten policy incl. opened-consumable rule; support empowered to refund up to a set amount without approval
Adverse reactionsDocumented protocol: intake script → immediate acknowledgment → escalation to the formulator → written record per incident → refund + follow-up. Never handled casually, never left in an inbox overnight
Shipping problemsTracking-based macros; reship-vs-refund rule by order value
EscalationOne named owner, one SLA (business-day response < 24h), one weekly support-themes line feeding the Monday summary

The Launch-Readiness Hit List (32 Items)

Owner-level checkboxes. An item is checked when its "done when" condition is met — not when it's been discussed.

A. Product & Scope

  • ☐The launch six named, in writing
  • ☐Retail + subscription price per SKU
  • ☐Landed COGS per SKU (margin computable)
  • ☐Packaging status per SKU: ready / dated / blocked
  • ☐Product data sheets complete per launch SKU
  • ☐Photography in hand, or budget + shoot date approved
  • ☐Formulator bio + headshot delivered

B. Money

  • ☐Build budget approved (site, apps, photography, legal review)
  • ☐Customer-acquisition test budget approved
  • ☐CAC ceiling set (derived from pricing + COGS)
  • ☐Margin floor set — below it, a SKU doesn't launch

C. Payments

  • ☐Seven payments-checklist answers in writing (above)

D. Operations & Support

  • ☐Fulfillment owner signed — in-house or named 3PL
  • ☐Returns/refund policy written
  • ☐Support owner named, hours + SLA set
  • ☐Adverse-reaction protocol documented

E. Legal

  • ☐Selling entity (merchant of record) confirmed
  • ☐Product liability insurance confirmed for these categories
  • ☐Claims-review owner named — signs every page and email
  • ☐Third-party brand agreement template drafted (before the first outside brand, including the pet line's terms)

F. Brand & Positioning

  • ☐Storefront name picked from the top five (domain/handle/trademark checks run same day)
  • ☐Primary customer defined in one sentence — who, what problem, what feeling
  • ☐Parent-store promise approved — why buy here instead of from each brand directly

G. Launch Authority

  • ☐Gate model adopted — launch on criteria, not dates
  • ☐Pause/scale authority assigned — who can halt paid spend or approve scaling without a meeting

Risk Register

  • Overexpansion (the named #1 risk): no seventh SKU, no new sector, no outside brand until the current phase's validation thresholds are met. The gates exist to enforce this.
  • Claims risk: methylene blue, kava, and cannabinoids all draw FDA/FTC attention and complicate ad approval. Mitigation: the claims guardrail file, the G2 gate, and an acquisition ladder that leans on email, affiliates, and organic before paid.
  • Payment fragility: the category invites processor trouble. Mitigation: the seven-answer checklist + a live backup processor.
  • Key-person dependency: the credibility story runs through one formulator. Mitigation: the seal + Science page make the method the asset; the masthead brand stands on its own.
