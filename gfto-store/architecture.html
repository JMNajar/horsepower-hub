Deep Dive · Category 02

Site Architecture

Four layers, four jobs: Home sells trust, sectors sell the problem, product pages sell the product, the Science pages close the skeptic.

← Back to the Working Brief

The Sitemap

Layer 1 — Home

The mistake to avoid: making Home a product grid. With a house of brands, a grid of 12 unrelated-looking labels reads like a flea market. Home's only job is to answer "why should I buy supplements from a company I've never heard of?" and route people by problem, not by brand.

  1. Hero — the story in one breath. "Every product here was formulated by one chemist, in one lab, with dosing you can verify." Mark at the bench. One CTA: "Find yours."
  2. The four sector doors. Big, visual, problem-labeled: Sharper days / Better nights / For your dog / [flex]. This is the primary navigation — the header just mirrors it.
  3. Trust bar. Lab-verified dosing badge, COA link, made-in-USA, the Mark Rhodes seal. The delivery technology's batch-precision data lives here in consumer form: "every batch tested, every dose exact."
  4. Meet the formulator strip. Three sentences + photo → About Mark. Masthead-level presence for Mark without naming the store after him.
  5. Email capture. Not a generic "subscribe" — a 60-second quiz: "which state are you chasing?" The quiz routes visitors to a sector AND segments the email list from day one.
  6. Footer: COAs, Science, FAQ, and the mandatory FDA structure/function disclaimer.

Layer 2 — Sector Pages (The Load-Bearing Layer)

Sector page ≠ paid-traffic landing page. The build gets both, and they're different artifacts. In-store sector pages (Shopify collections, dressed up) are shoppable, in the nav, and SEO-targeted at problem searches. Spoke landing pages (the free-trial offer pages) sit outside the browsing path — no nav, one offer, one CTA — and are generated per campaign by the AI layer.

Sector page template — the same skeleton × 4, so it's cheap to build and the AI can populate it:

BlockPurpose
Problem-first hero"Foggy afternoons?" not "Buy MB Fizz" — meets the visitor at the symptom
Product cards (2–4)Each in its own brand look, each with the Rhodes seal — comparison-friendly, with honest "which one is for you" guidance
"Why these work" stripSector-specific science teaser → links to The Science page
Cross-sector ribbon"Sharper days start with better nights →" — one per page, pointing at the adjacent sector; this is the on-page cross-promotion hook
Sector email captureSector-specific lead magnet (e.g., "The 7-day sleep reset") — tags the subscriber by sector in Klaviyo

The ribbon pairings matter: Mind & Energy ↔ Rest & Calm is the natural loop — you can't focus if you don't sleep, and the store sells both sides of the same day. Pet Wellness cross-sells from a different emotion ("you take care of your recovery — your dog deserves the same"), and post-purchase email is a better cross-sell channel for pets than on-page ribbons.

Layer 3 — Product / Brand Pages

The hard design problem of a house of brands in one store: each page must feel like its brand while unmistakably belonging to one trustworthy store. The solution is a fixed frame with a swappable skin:

  • Constant across all 12: page layout, store header/footer, COA/batch-link placement, subscribe-and-save module, review module, and the clickable "Formulated by Mark Rhodes" seal. These constants ARE the store brand.
  • Variable per product: palette, brand logo, photography style, copy voice. On Shopify this is one product template plus brand metafields (accent color, logo, brand blurb) — not 12 custom templates. That's what makes a 12-SKU launch survivable in an 8-week window.
  • Every page also carries: subscription-first pricing, an AI-selectable "pairs well with" cross-sell slot, and a sector breadcrumb back to its collection.

Layer 4 — About Mark / The Science

Two pages, deliberately separate, because they serve two different skeptics:

  • About Mark answers "who made this?" — biography, the bench, the why. Warm, photo-led. Every Rhodes seal on the site lands here.
  • The Science answers "does this actually work?" — the delivery-technology story rewritten for consumers: lead with the outcome of the tech (exact doses, faster absorption, batch-verified), show the dosing-precision data as a simple visual, and keep the whole explanation self-contained so a curious customer never needs to leave the site to understand it.

Compliance note: The Science page is where FDA claims risk concentrates (methylene blue and kava especially). Structure/function language only, disclaimer present, and this page goes through the claims-review gate before launch — it's the page a regulator or an ad-platform reviewer reads first.

Beyond the Sector Doors (New in v2)

Sector doors are the default path, not the only path. Some customers shop by outcome, ingredient, format, or search — v2 gives each of them a way in:

  • Site search — prominent, with product-type and ingredient synonyms configured ("MB," "methylene blue," "focus drink" all land correctly).
  • Shop by outcome — tag-driven collections: Focus · Sleep · Calm · Energy · Recovery. Same products, different entrance.
  • Shop by format — drink, gum, tear pack, treat: the low-cost form factors are impulse-friendly, and format is how convenience buyers think.
  • Comparison guides — "which one is for you" pages inside each sector, doing honest triage between the store's own products. These double as SEO assets.

The wholesale wing (gated, new in v2): a protected B2B portal — login-gated tiered pricing, case quantities, and reorder tooling for smoke shops, convenience stores, truck stops, and specialty retailers. It shares the product catalog but not the consumer pricing. Built when the wholesale gate opens (D2C unit economics proven), at which point a CRM (HubSpot Free) enters the stack for pipeline management. Designing the store with this wing in mind now costs nothing; retrofitting it later costs a re-architecture.

Decisions This Architecture Forces

  1. The flex sector can't be named until the final SKU list lands. Recovery products → "Body & Recovery"; general daily-health → "Daily Foundations." A wrong door confuses the whole nav — don't invent it in advance.
  2. Existing brand domains become marketing fronts, not separate stores. Recommendation: one checkout hub. Brand domains keep their story pages, but every "Buy" button routes into this store. One inventory, one analytics stream, one Klaviyo — the AI evaluation requirement effectively demands it.
  3. Kava placement is fault-tolerant. If payment processing for kava comes back restricted, Rest & Calm launches with Trypt.co alone and kava slots in later — no page rework needed. Sector-first design absorbs the hit.
  4. Launch narrow, architect wide. Phase 1 opens with ~6 SKUs in 2–3 sectors, but the nav, templates, and wholesale wing are designed for the full catalog — adding Phase 2 products is data entry, not redesign.

Where the AI Layer Plugs In

  • Home quiz → segments every subscriber by sector at capture
  • Sector pages → AI-generated education blocks refreshed on schedule; spoke pages generated per campaign and A/B rotated
  • Product pages → AI-selected "pairs well with" cross-sells from actual purchase-pair data
  • Evaluation → cross-sector purchase rate at 60 days is THE architecture-proving metric, tracked automatically
