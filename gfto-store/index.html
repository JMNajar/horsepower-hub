GFTO Multi-Product D2C Store

One store. Twelve brands. One formulator. The complete go-to-market working brief for a house-of-brands direct-to-consumer storefront built on Mark Rhodes' formulation work and DeeperGreen delivery technology.

The Thesis

GF Tech One holds roughly 12 formulated, ready-to-sell products — LEUCO Methylene Blue Fizz, Trypt.co Tryptophan, a kava relaxation product, a premium dog treat line, and more — that share one common denominator: every one was formulated by Mark Rhodes, and most run on DeeperGreen, a delivery technology with lab-verified dosing precision.

The play is direct-to-consumer first: one Shopify storefront selling all ~12 products as a house of brands (each product keeps its own identity), organized into four problem-based sectors, powered by an AI automation layer that markets, promotes, and evaluates the program with minimal human time. B2B formulation services become an uplift once D2C proves viable.

Why one store beats twelve: acquiring a customer costs $20–50 in paid channels; emailing an existing customer costs ~$0. A shared customer list, shared purchase history, and cross-sector email ladders make the second purchase nearly pure margin. Twelve separate stores physically can't do this. That's the moat.

Category 01

Storefront Naming

The store needs its own masthead brand — one that holds twelve product brands underneath it without competing with them. The naming work ran three rounds across eight lanes; the formulator's name stays off the masthead (trademark exposure + key-man risk) and instead becomes a "Formulated by Mark Rhodes" seal carried on every product page. The five finalists:

NameWhy It Made the Cut
1Open Road Supply Co.Freedom and journey language, native to the motorcycle and lifestyle audiences that make up the first warm lists. The "Supply Co." frame is built to hold multiple brands — a supply company naturally carries many labels.
2Even KeelThe only finalist that translates the science claim into a feeling. DeeperGreen's proof point is dosing consistency — Even Keel says what consistency feels like: steady energy, steady sleep, steady calm. Strongest pure brand idea on the board.
3Bench & BottleLab bench to your bottle — craft warmth with visible lab DNA. Elastic across human and pet products, and pre-built for the future B2B story ("our bench, your brand").
4Placer Supply Co.The most ownable story: the products are formulated in Auburn, CA — seat of Placer County, where placer literally means gold-bearing ground. "We mine the good stuff" without saying it. The trade-off: it requires teaching pronunciation (PLASS-er).
5Foothill Supply Co.The safe, solid regional pick — Sierra foothills geography that's real, warm, and rugged. Lowest risk, lowest ceiling of the five.

Category 02

Site Architecture

Four layers, four different jobs: Home sells trust, sectors sell the problem, product pages sell the product, and the Science/Formulator pages close the skeptic.

  • Home — the Mark Rhodes / DeeperGreen story compressed into one breath, then four problem-labeled sector doors. Home routes by problem, never by brand — a grid of 12 unrelated labels reads like a flea market.
  • Four sector pages — Mind & Energy · Rest & Calm · Pet Wellness · one flex sector. Problem-first education, 2–4 product cards each, and a cross-sector ribbon that starts the cross-promotion loop.
  • Twelve product/brand pages — a fixed frame with a swappable skin: constant layout, COA links, subscribe-and-save, and the Rhodes seal; variable colors, logo, photography, and voice per brand.
  • About Mark / The Science — two pages for two skeptics: "who made this?" and "does it actually work?" The DeeperGreen story rewritten for consumers, kept one click deep.

Category 03

Offer Model — Hub & Spoke

The hub retains; the spokes acquire; the email engine multiplies.

  • Hub: the Shopify store — straight retail plus subscribe-and-save on every consumable. A ~22% subscription discount buys roughly 4x lifetime value. This is where the business model lives.
  • Spokes: one free-trial / sample landing page per sector — not per product. Four spokes concentrate ad spend, map to how the warm audiences actually think (by problem, not by SKU), and stay cheap to regenerate per campaign.
  • Cross-promotion engine: post-purchase email ladders route buyers across sectors — bought sleep? Thirty days later, a sharper-mornings offer. The KPI that proves the whole architecture: cross-sector purchase rate at 60 days.

Category 04

AI Automation Layer

The hard requirement: the program must market, promote, and evaluate itself in an automated fashion. Three functions, unequal builds — Market is a weekly loop, Promote is build-once, Evaluate is built like software.

  • Market: an AI content engine batches every week's sector emails and social drafts by Monday 9am — human-approved in one ~30-minute session, claims-checked before a person ever sees them, then scheduled automatically.
  • Promote: Klaviyo flows — welcome, abandoned cart, post-purchase subscription conversion, cross-sector ladders, predictive winback — built once, running forever.
  • Evaluate: a Sunday-night cron pulls Shopify and Klaviyo data into a live KPI dashboard, and an AI-written Monday summary lands in leadership's inbox at 6am — revenue, CAC, repeat rate, and the best cross-sell path per sector, with one decision flagged when one is needed.
  • Total stack cost: ~$50/month. The whole layer runs on about 30 minutes of human time a week.

Category 05

Tech Stack

Every platform choice was scored against this build's real requirements — 12-brand templating, native subscriptions, supplement-friendly payments, API depth for the AI layer, and a 1–2 month launch window — not against generic feature lists.

  • Shopify Basic (8.6/10) — wins on AI/API ecosystem, house-of-brands templating speed, and free native subscriptions. One flagged risk, quantified and absorbable.
  • Klaviyo (9.0/10) — the cross-promotion engine is literally a Klaviyo-native feature set: sector segments, product-triggered flows, predicted next-order dates.
  • OuterSignal — Shopify app that enriches each buyer with public social data: spots influencers in the order stream and feeds real customer demographics into Klaviyo segments.
  • Base stack: ~$100–110/month before ad spend.

Category 06

Timeline & Dependencies

Full build in 8 weeks — all ~12 products at launch, with the automation layer installed before opening day, not bolted on after.

  • Weeks 1–2: Foundation — Shopify build, brand voice files, Klaviyo flows.
  • Weeks 3–4: Product and sector pages; content engine running on the pre-launch list.
  • Weeks 5–6: Sector spoke pages; KPI dashboard on live test data.
  • Week 7: Compliance claims review, QA, payment testing.
  • Week 8: Launch — warm lists first, paid traffic second.
  • Critical path items: final SKU list and pricing, product photography, Mark Rhodes bio and headshot, the kava payment-processor confirmation, and pet-brand partnership terms.
