GFTO Multi-Product D2C Store

One store, launched narrow and built to scale: six proven SKUs first, a house of brands behind one trust story — every product formulated by Mark Rhodes — governed by decision gates, powered by an AI engine that markets, promotes, and evaluates itself.

The Thesis (v2)

GF Tech One holds a portfolio of formulated, ready-to-sell wellness products — methylene blue, tryptophan, kava, premium pet treats, and low-cost effect-driven form factors like infused sugar, tear packs, and gum — sharing one common denominator: every product was formulated by Mark Rhodes, most on a delivery technology with lab-verified dosing precision.

The v2 model is margin-first and deliberately narrow: launch approximately six sellable SKUs that are already packaged or nearly complete, on one Shopify storefront organized by the effect the customer wants. Validate demand, repeat purchase, compliance, and unit economics — then expand: remaining SKUs in Phase 2, a protected wholesale portal (smoke shops, convenience, truck stops, specialty retail) at its own revenue gate, and a marketplace phase adding third-party brands under revenue-share once the platform proves it can sell.

Why one store beats many: acquiring a customer costs $20–50 in paid channels; emailing an existing customer costs ~$0. A shared customer list, shared purchase history, and cross-sector email ladders make the second purchase nearly pure margin. Separate stores physically can't do this. That's the moat — and the named enemy of this plan is overexpansion.

The operating model is the edge: the entire program — content, campaigns, cross-sell, and the weekly scoreboard — runs on an AI engine with one 30-minute human approval session per week. The tool stack starts at roughly $100/month and unlocks new spending only at revenue milestones, falling to under 1% of revenue at scale. Every Monday at 6am, the store reports its own numbers — revenue, repeat rate, best cross-sell path per sector — with one decision flagged when one is needed. And the engine starts four weeks before the store opens, so day one arrives with a segmented audience instead of a cold start.

The customer promise: "For people who want to feel a specific way — sharper, calmer, better-rested — and are done gambling on internet supplements: every product here does one job, at a verified dose, from one formulator you can look in the eye."

Category 01

Storefront Naming

The store needs its own masthead brand — one that holds every product brand underneath it without competing with them. The naming work ran three rounds across eight lanes; the formulator's name stays off the masthead (trademark exposure + key-man risk) and instead becomes a "Formulated by Mark Rhodes" seal carried on every product page. The five finalists:

NameWhy It Made the Cut
1Open Road Supply Co.Freedom and journey language, native to the motorcycle and lifestyle audiences that make up the first warm lists. The "Supply Co." frame is built to hold multiple brands — a supply company naturally carries many labels.
2Even KeelThe only finalist that translates the science claim into a feeling. The technology's proof point is dosing consistency — Even Keel says what consistency feels like: steady energy, steady sleep, steady calm. Strongest pure brand idea on the board.
3Bench & BottleLab bench to your bottle — craft warmth with visible lab DNA. Elastic across human and pet products, and pre-built for the wholesale and marketplace phases ("our bench, your brand").
4Placer Supply Co.The most ownable story: the products are formulated in Auburn, CA — seat of Placer County, where placer literally means gold-bearing ground. "We mine the good stuff" without saying it. The trade-off: it requires teaching pronunciation (PLASS-er).
5Foothill Supply Co.The safe, solid regional pick — Sierra foothills geography that's real, warm, and rugged. Lowest risk, lowest ceiling of the five.

Category 02

Site Architecture

Four layers, four different jobs: Home sells trust, sectors sell the problem, product pages sell the product, and the Science/Formulator pages close the skeptic. V2 adds the paths beyond the sector doors and the wholesale wing.

  • Home — the Mark Rhodes story compressed into one breath, then problem-labeled sector doors. Home routes by the effect the customer wants, never by brand.
  • Sector pages — Mind & Energy · Rest & Calm · Pet Wellness · one flex sector (named when the final SKU list lands). Problem-first education, product cards, and a cross-sector ribbon that starts the cross-promotion loop.
  • Beyond the doors (new in v2): site search, shop-by-outcome, shop-by-format (drink, gum, tear pack, treat), and comparison guides — because not every customer thinks in sectors.
  • Product/brand pages — a fixed frame with a swappable skin: constant layout, COA links, subscribe-and-save, and the Rhodes seal; variable brand look per product.
  • About Mark / The Science — two pages for two skeptics: "who made this?" and "does it actually work?" — kept one click deep, effect-first.
  • Wholesale wing (gated, new in v2) — a protected B2B portal with tiered pricing for smoke shops, convenience stores, truck stops, and specialty retail. Built when the wholesale gate opens, not at launch.

Category 03

Offer Model — Hub & Spoke, Phased

The hub retains; the spokes acquire; the email engine multiplies — and v2 sequences it in phases with a channel-by-channel acquisition ladder.

  • Phase 1 (launch): ~6 SKUs across 2–3 sectors. Straight retail + subscribe-and-save on every consumable (~22% subscription discount ≈ 4x lifetime value). Sampling offers on each active sector's spoke page.
  • Phase 2: remaining owned SKUs + the fourth sector — unlocked by Phase 1 hitting its validation thresholds, not by the calendar.
  • Phase 3: wholesale portal, then third-party brands under revenue-share — the store becomes a distribution asset.
  • Acquisition ladder (in order): warm lists → affiliates (the existing associate-commission network) → organic social + influencer seeding → paid traffic last, each channel with its own budget and success threshold.
  • Cross-promotion engine: post-purchase email ladders route buyers across sectors. The KPI that proves the architecture: cross-sector purchase rate at 60 days.

Category 04

AI Automation Layer

The hard requirement: the program must market, promote, and evaluate itself in an automated fashion. Three functions, unequal builds — Market is a weekly loop, Promote is build-once, Evaluate is built like software.

  • Market: an AI content engine batches every week's sector emails and social drafts by Monday 9am — claims-checked before a human ever sees them, approved in one ~30-minute session, then scheduled automatically.
  • Promote: Klaviyo flows — welcome, abandoned cart, post-purchase subscription conversion, cross-sector ladders, predictive winback — built once, running forever.
  • Evaluate: a Sunday-night cron pulls Shopify, Klaviyo, and enrichment data into a live KPI dashboard, and an AI-written Monday summary lands in leadership's inbox at 6am — revenue, CAC, repeat rate, best cross-sell path per sector, one decision flagged when one is needed.
  • The v2 standing rule: no tool enters the stack unless the AI can drive it through an API. Every layer of the store is wired so the engine can read it and act on it.

Category 06

Gates, Timeline & Launch Readiness

Eight weeks is the floor, and it's only real if packaging, claims, photography, inventory, processor approval, fulfillment, and product data are substantially complete before the clock starts. V2 replaces date-authority with five decision gates — the calendar estimates; the gates decide.

  • G0 — Start gate: launch SKUs named, pricing + COGS, packaging status, product data sheets, photography, formulator bio in hand. Until G0 passes, the build clock hasn't started.
  • G1 — Build gate: name locked; the full payments checklist answered in writing (per-SKU eligibility, reserves, chargeback rules, subscription billing, state restrictions, shipping lanes, backup processor); fulfillment owner signed.
  • G2 — Content gate: claims sign-off on every page and flow email; the support system live — owner, SLA, refund policy, and an adverse-reaction protocol. Wellness products require a defined response system before anything publishes.
  • G3 — Soft launch: warm lists only. Advance threshold: ≥1.5% list-to-purchase and <2% refund rate over 14 days.
  • G4 — Paid traffic: one sector, 3–5 creatives, defined daily budget, 14 days. Scale when CAC beats the ceiling two weeks running; pause when CAC exceeds 1.5× ceiling for 7 days.
  • The readiness hit list: 32 owner-level checkboxes across product, money, payments, operations, legal, brand, and launch authority — the deep dive carries the full list.

Category 07

The Marketing Engine

Marketing isn't a list of tactics — it's one weekly loop: AI does the volume, data picks the winners, a human guards the claims. About one hour of human time a week runs the whole portfolio.

  • Four subsystems: Audience (quiz + enrichment segments everyone automatically) → Content (one hero piece per sector cascades into ~12 assets) → Distribution (owned and earned channels first, paid last) → Learning (last week's numbers write next week's brief).
  • The weekly rhythm: Sunday-night generation → Monday 6am scoreboard → one 30-minute Tuesday approval → everything ships automatically all week.
  • Channel map + AI search visibility (AEO) live on the Tech Stack page — email/push engine room, clean-lane SMS, YouTube and SEO as compounding assets, formulator podcast circuit, paid Meta last.
  • The pre-launch play: the engine starts 4 weeks before the store opens — day one arrives with a segmented list, not a cold start.
