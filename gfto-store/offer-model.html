Offer Model — Hub & Spoke

The hub retains, the spokes acquire, and the cross-promotion engine is why twelve brands in one store beat twelve separate stores.

The customer promise (fixing v1's product-centric gap): "For people who want to feel a specific way — sharper, calmer, better-rested — and are done gambling on internet supplements: every product here does one job, at a verified dose, from one formulator you can look in the eye." Marketing leads with the effect, the convenience, the price, and the ritual — never the technical complexity.

Why buy from the parent store instead of each brand directly: one checkout and one subscription across every brand; one verified-COA standard behind everything; cross-brand bundles available nowhere else; one accountable support line. The store isn't a middleman — it's the trust layer.

The Hub — Where LTV Lives

The hub's economics run on one conversion: one-time buyer → subscriber. Everything in the store design serves that switch.

  • Every consumable gets two prices, subscription listed first. The proven ladder: ~$44.95 one-time / ~$34.95 subscribe — roughly a 20–25% subscription discount applied across the catalog. The discount isn't generosity; it's buying predictable revenue.
  • The math in one line: a $34.95 subscriber at a 5-month average retention is ~$175 of lifetime value versus $45 for the one-time buyer — 4x LTV for a 22% discount.
  • Subscription framing by sector, not by product: "Never run out of sharp days." Consumables with a felt effect have a natural reorder emotion — the copy attaches the subscription to the state, not the bottle.
  • Bundles: one per sector ("The Full Night") and one cross-sector flagship ("The 24-Hour Stack"). Bundles raise average order value 15–30% and pre-seed the cross-sector behavior the email engine exploits.

The Spokes — One Per Sector, Not Per Product

Each spoke is a standalone free-sample / trial-size landing page: no navigation, one offer, one CTA, checkout dumping into the hub with the buyer tagged by sector. Three data-backed reasons it's per-sector:

  1. Ad economics. Twelve product spokes means twelve testing cycles' worth of learning-phase ad spend. Four sector spokes concentrate budget so each page exits the ad platforms' learning phase months sooner.
  2. Warm audiences think in problems, not SKUs. The existing warm lists map cleanly to sectors — lifestyle audiences to Mind & Energy, the same plus sleep-seekers to Rest & Calm, pet communities to Pet Wellness. Nobody on those lists is searching for a brand name they've never heard.
  3. Maintenance. Spokes are disposable test surfaces the AI layer regenerates per campaign. Four templates is a sustainable regeneration loop; twelve is not.

The sample offer is really list acquisition with a qualifier. Someone who pays $4.95 shipping for a free sample is worth more than ten email subscribers who paid nothing — they've demonstrated purchase intent, an address, and a working card.

The Cross-Promotion Engine

The math that justifies the whole architecture: acquiring a customer costs $20–50 in paid channels; emailing an existing customer costs approximately zero. The second-sector purchase is nearly pure margin — but only if all twelve brands share one customer list, one purchase history, and one email system. Twelve separate stores physically can't do this.

Why this order: each rung generates the conversion data that de-risks the next. Warm lists prove the offer; affiliates prove it travels; organic proves the creative angles; paid then spends against evidence instead of guesses. Ad-platform restrictions on this product category also make rungs 1–3 the dependable spine, not a warm-up act.
