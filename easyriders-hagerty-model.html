Companion Brief

The Hagerty Model

What Hagerty actually built, the conflict-of-interest critique aimed at it, and the fork in the road Easyriders should not copy.

Hagerty proved the core insight behind the Easyriders membership strategy — bundle media, perks, and belonging into one paid membership, and loyalty does the selling. But Hagerty's own growth path shows exactly where that model creates a real liability. Easyriders should take the insight and route around the liability.

What Hagerty actually built

Hagerty started as a Midwest, family-owned specialty insurer for classic and collector vehicles. Around 2017–2019, CEO McKeel Hagerty repositioned the whole company from "insurance provider" to membership organization: the Hagerty Drivers Club ($45/year) bundles the magazine, valuation tools, roadside assistance, event access, and discounts — and critically, removed the requirement to hold a Hagerty insurance policy to join at all. Anyone "of driving age" who's interested in cars can become a member.

The data point that matters most for this strategy: Gen X and Millennials now out-query Boomers for Hagerty valuations and quotes — direct evidence the "younger generations don't care about cars" assumption is wrong for exactly the two generations the Easyriders playbook targets in Section 02: The Generational Landscape.

Since its 2021 NYSE listing (HGTY) gave it real acquisition capital, Hagerty has systematically bought the infrastructure around the hobby: event properties (Greenwich Concours, California Mille, the Concours d'Elegance of America, the Amelia Island Concours), grassroots registration software (MotorsportReg, later resold), youth-oriented car culture (Radwood), dealer and auction back-end technology (Speed Digital, which now powers 249+ sites including direct competitors), a controlling and then full stake in Broad Arrow Auctions, and — as of a $18.4M cash deal in September 2024 — its own insurance underwriter, Consolidated National Insurance Company. That last move closed a loop: Hagerty now sets the valuations, runs the auctions, owns the events, and underwrites the policy, all under one roof.

The critique — and who's making it

A competing marketplace founder (Rudy Samsel of GuysWithRides) has published a widely-discussed video essay arguing this amounts to an "oligarchy": the same company that sets classic-car values through its own price guide also runs the auctions that test those values, owns the events where the auctions happen, and now underwrites the insurance — a closed loop with no independent check. He cites an anonymous former Hagerty employee, reported by Bloomberg: "They're setting the values, they're having the auctions, they have an online marketplace... they're telling people what to pay for stuff, telling people what to buy next."

Read this claim correctlySamsel discloses upfront that he tried to build a competing online auction platform and reached out to Hagerty for advertising before being quoted a $30,000/year print-bundle requirement — months before Hagerty launched its own competing marketplace. That's a real, disclosed conflict of interest on the critic's side, not a neutral third party. The underlying acquisitions are documented, real transactions. The "oligarchy" framing is his editorial interpretation of what they add up to, not a regulatory or legal finding. Both things are true at once: the critique is self-interested, and the structural risk it describes is real.

Why this matters for Easyriders — and where the fork is

Easyriders is looking at the same underlying insight Hagerty proved — bundle media, perks, and belonging into one membership and let loyalty sell it — while standing at a real fork in the road:

PathWhat it looks likeThe risk
A — Copy the full stackEasyriders sets its own custom-bike valuation guide, runs its own auctions, owns regional bike shows, eventually underwrites its own insurance.Imports a conflict-of-interest exposure Easyriders doesn't currently have, in a market that doesn't reward it the way investment-grade classic-car auctions do.
B — Take the membership, skip the vertical integrationBundle media + events + community + a partnered, not owned, insurance/roadside perk.Requires giving up some margin to a partner insurer — the trade-off for staying out of the "who's checking the checker" problem entirely.
Recommendation: Path BMotorcycle culture — especially the outlaw/custom/chopper world Easyriders covers — isn't investment-grade the way concours-level classic cars are. There's no equivalent price-guide battle to win, so Easyriders doesn't carry Hagerty's biggest conflict-of-interest exposure to begin with, and manufacturing one (an "Easyriders Bike Value Guide") would import risk for a market that doesn't pay for it. Easyriders' real moat — established in Section 07: Competitive Advantage — is the 55-year archive and real relationships with builders, artists, and legends: culture and access, not financial infrastructure. The concrete move: partner, don't underwrite. License an Easyriders-branded insurance and roadside-assistance benefit through an existing specialty motorcycle insurer — the way Hagerty itself started, before it became its own underwriter — and get the membership glue without ever becoming the insurer, the appraiser, and the auction house at once.
